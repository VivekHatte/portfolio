Orbital Ledger

A distributed, event-sourced ledger that handles 2M+ events per second with sub-15 ms p99 reads. Built to retire a legacy database that was buckling under hot-row contention and audit replay times measured in hours.

Status Shipped
Year 2024
Role Lead Engineer
Team 4 Engineers
Live System Source

Mission brief.

The product had outgrown its single-writer Postgres ledger. Every payment, refund, and reconciliation hit the same hot rows, and our nightly audit job needed eight hours to replay a single day of activity. We had two months before the next regulatory checkpoint.

The mandate: rebuild the ledger as the system of record, keep zero data loss during cutover, and prove the new design under a synthetic 10x peak.

The problem.

Hot-row contention. The top 200 merchant accounts produced 80% of traffic. Every transaction held a row lock on a balance row, serializing throughput and capping us at roughly 1,200 writes per second.

Replay was destructive. Auditors wanted to re-derive any balance at any point in time. The existing schema mutated rows in place; reconstructing state required parsing tens of millions of triggered audit rows for a single account.

Region failover was theoretical. A regional outage would have meant a manual failover with several minutes of write loss. Compliance considered this unacceptable for the next audit cycle.

The approach.

We split the write path from the read path. Writes became immutable events on a partitioned Kafka topic keyed by account, giving us linear scale and a natural audit trail. Reads were served from projections rebuilt by a stateless projector service, with hot balances cached in Redis behind a write-through invalidation hook.

Snapshots were taken every 10k events per account, so a fresh projector could rejoin and catch up in seconds instead of hours. The whole pipeline ran multi-region active with a CRDT-based reconciliation step for the rare cross-region conflicts.

Mission log.

  1. WEEK 01 — 02

    Discovery + Schema Design

    Mapped the legacy schema, profiled hot rows, drafted the event grammar with stakeholders. Ran a one-day spike to validate Kafka partition strategy.

  2. WEEK 03 — 05

    Dual-Write Cutover

    Shipped a shim that wrote to both the legacy DB and the new event stream. Built the projector and balance cache and ran continuous reconciliation against legacy reads.

  3. WEEK 06 — 07

    Load Testing + Tuning

    Ran 10x synthetic peaks, tuned Kafka producer batching and Redis pipelining. Knocked p99 from 78 ms down to 14 ms. Validated multi-region failover.

  4. WEEK 08

    Cutover + Decommission

    Flipped reads to the new system region-by-region with a 60-second rollback window. Decommissioned the legacy writer two weeks later. Zero data loss, zero customer-visible downtime.
